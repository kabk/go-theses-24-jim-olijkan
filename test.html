As we just concluded, each act of framing is the result of an active choice (though not necessarily deliberate or conscious). Because of this, we can assu(fig. 3) me that whatever is framed, holds some degree of importance to the framer. A grandparent framing a school portrait of their grandchild is an obvious example. The grandparent probably won't care much, whether the photo turned out well, because their grandchild is important to them.

With this logic, we can look at the act of framing as a ritual that bestows a certain "true-ness" and authority over the framed. But don't be fooled, this authority is only a projected reality of the author. In this thesis I will explore frames in visual media: The ones found in museums and galleries, those in books and editorial design. And the frames found in online-culture and memes. I want to investigate the history and traditions of frames, and I want to discover how they relate to more abstract frames, like certain social constructs and topics relating to identity.

I will give a general introduction to frames, dissecting a frame into its properties and functions. After this introduction, this thesis will continue following a non-linear structure consisting of little standalone vig(fig. 3)nettes. These little "nuggets" are not meant to be read in any specific order.
